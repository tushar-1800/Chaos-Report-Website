Understanding the CHAOS Report

A modern look at software project success and failure, and the decisive role of project management.

About the CHAOS Report

The CHAOS Report, published by the Standish Group, is the most influential research on software project outcomes. Since 1994, it has tracked the rates of project success, challenge, and failure across decades, providing a benchmark for the IT industry.[1]

Key findings: In 1994, only 16% of projects succeeded, while 31% failed outright. By 2020, the success rate improved to 31%, but half of all projects were still challenged, and 19% failed.[2]

Why does this matter? The report highlights that technology alone doesn't guarantee success—leadership, communication, and stakeholder engagement are critical. The CHAOS Report is a call to action for organizations to rethink how they approach software projects, emphasizing the importance of people and process over just tools and code.[3]

Understanding these factors can help organizations avoid costly mistakes and deliver better results. The CHAOS Report remains a vital resource for anyone involved in software development or project management.

CHAOS Report Summaries & Trends

Year % Successful % Challenged % Failed Summary
1994 16.2% 52.7% 31.1% The original CHAOS Report shocked the industry by revealing that only 16% of software projects were successful, while over half were challenged and nearly a third failed. This report brought global attention to the "software crisis" and highlighted the need for better project management and stakeholder involvement.[2]
2006 35% 46% 19% By 2006, the success rate had more than doubled, reflecting improvements in project management practices and methodologies. However, nearly half of projects were still challenged, showing that fundamental issues persisted.[4]
2009 32% 44% 24% The 2009 report showed a slight decline in success rates, with a notable increase in failed projects. This period highlighted the challenges of scaling agile practices and the ongoing need for strong leadership.[5]
2012 37% 42% 21% In 2012, the success rate reached its highest point, but the majority of projects were still not fully successful. The report emphasized the importance of executive support and clear requirements.[6]
2020 31% 50% 19% The latest available CHAOS Report shows that while outright failures have decreased, only about a third of projects are truly successful. The focus has shifted to the role of leadership, team dynamics, and organizational culture as the main drivers of success.[7]

What changed? Over time, the CHAOS Reports have shown that technical solutions alone are not enough. The most successful projects have strong sponsors, engaged teams, and clear goals. The evolution of the report's findings reflects the growing recognition that people, leadership, and adaptability are at the heart of project success.[8]

Deeper insight:
The CHAOS Report is not just a set of statistics. It is a call to action for organizations to rethink how they approach software projects. By learning from decades of data, teams can avoid repeating the same mistakes and instead build a culture of continuous improvement and leadership. The report's influence is seen in the widespread adoption of agile, the focus on value delivery, and the rise of project management as a strategic discipline.[9]

Direct CHAOS Report Links & Summaries

1995 CHAOS Report

The original CHAOS Report (1995) by the Standish Group was groundbreaking, revealing that only 16.2% of software projects were successful, 52.7% were challenged (over budget, late, or lacking features), and 31.1% failed outright. This report brought global attention to the "software crisis," highlighting the urgent need for better project management, user involvement, and executive support.

2020 CHAOS Report

The 2020 CHAOS Report showed improvement, with 31% of projects classified as successful, 50% as challenged, and 19% as failed. While outright failures decreased, most projects still struggled to meet all objectives. The report emphasized that leadership, team dynamics, and organizational culture are now the main drivers of project success, rather than just technical factors.

Why Projects Succeed or Fail

Leadership matters: The difference between success and failure often comes down to the project manager's ability to lead, communicate, and adapt. The CHAOS Reports consistently show that projects with strong leadership, clear vision, and active stakeholder involvement are far more likely to succeed.[11]

Common pitfalls: Poor requirements, lack of executive support, and weak stakeholder engagement are recurring themes in failed projects. Many organizations underestimate the complexity of software projects and fail to invest in the skills and processes needed for success.[12]

Modern approaches:
Research shows that agile methods, empowered teams, and a focus on delivering value—not just features—are key to improving outcomes. Organizations that invest in project leadership and continuous learning see much higher rates of success. The CHAOS Report's findings have inspired a shift toward adaptive, people-centered project management.[13]

Looking forward:
As technology evolves, the lessons of the CHAOS Report remain relevant. Success in software projects will always depend on the ability to manage change, foster collaboration, and maintain a relentless focus on delivering real business value.[14]
